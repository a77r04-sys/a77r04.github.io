Rover End Effector

As part of the Cornell Mars Rover team, I designed and built an end effector mounted onto the rover's arm. This system is critical for picking up rocks of complicated geometry, carrying up to 5 kg, and completing dexterous tasks such as typing, screwing, and flipping switches.

01. First Prototype

The first prototype of the end effector utilized a four-linkage system and a DC motor. The design incorporated a camera and a self-actuating screwdriver mechanism to assist with equipment servicing tasks.

Outcome: Unfortunately, the prototype had too much total weight, and the 3D printed components could not sustain the stress of carrying the maximum required load of 5kg. This failure led to a complete redesign of the gripping mechanics.

The four-linkage system (Prototype 1)

02. Final Prototype

The final prototype utilized a linear actuator and springs to create a gripping mechanism that could "bend in" more, allowing the end effector to improve its grip on irregular rocks.

Additionally, the end effector employed TPU (flexible) grippers to better adjust to the rugged edges of rocks. The end effector had both a camera and a laser to make it easier for the controls team to align the arm. To save weight, the self-actuating screwdriver was removed in favor of the improved gripping capability.
